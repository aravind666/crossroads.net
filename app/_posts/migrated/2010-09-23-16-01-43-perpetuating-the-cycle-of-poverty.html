In A Framework for Understanding Poverty, Dr. Ruby Payne defines poverty as the “extent to which one does without resources.” These resources are not just financial; they relate to a person’s emotional well-being, mental abilities/education, support systems, relationships, and more.

 

Most of us don’t think about our lives in terms of resources—but we all have a specific measure of them; and we belong to a specific economic culture based on those resources and the shared beliefs, norms and practices that correspond with them. These cultural practices guide us and provide a value system that we pass along to our families, perpetuating practices for generations to come.

 

How can a specific resource level influence a cultural practice/value? Let’s look at one example: Financial resource is defined by having the money to purchase goods and services. If you have limited income, your primary objective is to pay for your basic needs: food, clothing, housing. If there is literally not enough money remaining—then there is limited opportunity or reason to practice money management skills, and therefore, little to no transference of those skills to your family. What you practice becomes a norm generationally unless there is an intervening relationship, resource or opportunity that introduces a new way of thinking and practice.

 

Now consider a broader view on the impact of growing up with limited financial resources: With limited financial resources, someone in poverty may not have built financial management skills and may have developed mistrust of banking/financial institutions based on inexperience. He may not have had a relationship with an adult who had a salaried job, and thus does not understand the practical importance of education and skill development for career employment. Career advancement is based on long-term patterns of employment, and if there are little to no family members with career jobs—then he may not have developed practical value for a consistent work history which can impact overall income.
