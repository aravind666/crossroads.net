South Africa is a country rich in natural resources (one of the wealthiest countries in Africa) and is quite modernized compared to its neighbors. Heck, the country successfully hosted the World Cup this past summer.So, why does a country like this need our help?

The answer is that despite its many advantages, decades of brutal, exploitative Apartheid rule have left South Africa with an avalanche of poverty and health problems. Yes, parts of South Africa are on par with the West in terms of quality of life. But, though Apartheid ended almost 20 years ago, the country is still destructively segregated; no longer by law, but by economic, social and health issues.

Parts of South Africa have incredible wealth, but the majority has been left to live in extreme poverty.

Consider these statistics:

  • The average life expectancy for a male in South Africa has decreased over the last 20 years from 70 to 47 years of age.In the U.S., the average life expectancy for a male is 75 years of age.
  • In South Africa, 59 children under age five die per 1,000 live births (compared to eight in the U.S.), making it one of only 12 countries in which this number has consistently increased since the early 90s.Thats almost 75,000 children dying each year.
  • Rates for diabetes, heart disease, cancer, kidney disease and mental illness are two to three times higher than the average for developing countries.
  • South Africa has 17% of the worlds HIV/AIDS cases5.5 million people.This is 23 times the global average. Over 350,000 new people die annually, and around 1,350 people are infected every day.
  • Tuberculosis case notifications in South Africa have risen four-fold since 1986, and the infection rate is seven times the global average.
  • The injury death rate is almost double the global average, and nearly half are caused by interpersonal violence, mostly between men.

The health problems in South Africa are HUGE (and its safe to say the numbers are even worse for Mamelodi specifically). Unfortunately, the public health system is wholly inadequate.South Africa spends more money on health care than many countries, but over 60% goes to the private sector, which only affects 14% of the population.Most people in Mamelodi do not have access to this care, and we, the Church, are not going to wait for the government to get the necessary care to those who are dying.

Ill end with a quote from a recent report on South Africa done by The Economist; "On taking over as minister of health a year ago, Aaron Motsoaledi declared himself shocked by the state of the public health-care system. Media horror stories about dirty and overcrowded hospitals, long waiting times, lack of medicines and a shortage of medical staff were largely true, he admitted: I dont think it will be an exaggeration to say that some of our hospitals are death traps."

Yes, there are people in the U. S. suffering, and our priority is to care for them (thats what CityLink is all about), but, if Jesus commanded us to care for the sick (without specifying geographical lines), can we ignore a place where people are dying at unthinkable rates, with no real hope for things to get better?

*Most of the information in this post comes from a report on Health in South Africa in The Lancet.
