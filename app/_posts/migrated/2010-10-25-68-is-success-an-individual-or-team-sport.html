Theres a common belief that achievement is only about the individuals effort, pulling ourselves up by our bootstraps, working harder, making our own way. This same expectation is often applied to people in povertythat simply applying more effort individually should be enough to overcome a tough situation.

Yet in Malcolm Gladwells book Outliers: The Story of Success, he writes, that weve been far too focused on the individualon describing the characteristics and habits and personality traits of those who get furthest ahead in the world....(W)hen outliers become outliers it is not just because of their own efforts. Its because of the contributions of lots of different people and lots of different circumstances.

If this is true of the most successful people, what is true for those who are facing significant barriers and limited resources? According to poverty expert Dr. Ruby Payne, one of the most important resources needed to move from poverty to self-sufficiency is a strong support system. But what about personal effort? Its still an essential component to personal advancement; however, its not the only factor.

Consider the story of Olympic gold medalist and world-class swimmer Michael Phelps: Because swimming is primarily an individual sport, Michael Phelps accomplishments are purely his own. But how did he become such an exceptional swimmer, and what contributed to his success?

It started with a combination of natural talent and hard work, but beyond that there were other contributors. His mother was able to access opportunities at local swim leagues, where he was then connected to coaches and trainers who built his confidence and refined his skills. These coaches had expertise and knowledge about being an Olympian, as well as connections to facilities and resources that built his skills over time. And even Michaels teammates played a role, providing him with a competitive atmosphere that pushed him to perform. In the end, his success was the result of both personal effort and community support.

Moving from poverty to self-sufficiency requires the same combinationit takes a personal commitment as well as a supportive community. Thats how CityLink Center can change the game, not only by integrating social services, but also by providing mentors and coaches who can help individuals break the cycle of generational poverty.
