CSM student Ben von Korff traveled to India with a small team from Crossroads earlier this year. They visited aftercare homes in Mumbai and Kolkata as part of their trip. In this entry, Ben shares his story of finding hope in a hopeless situation.

On August 3, 2010, our team visited a government-run aftercare home in Mumbai. This is the place where a girl rescued from human trafficking goes to begin her rehabilitation process. As we entered this place it was reasonable to assume that we would be experiencing something that would bring us down in spirit. I at least believed that we were going to witness a seeming bleakness and hopelessness. God however, had something else in mind for us. Here is what happened upon entering the home:

Twelve of us entered a room with a cold concrete floor and florescent lighting. As we looked across the room seventy beautiful girls returned equally inquisitive looks. We proceeded to sit in front of the girls where we would play a song. Scott Dill, one of our team members, announced that he would play a song from a Bollywood movie, Three Idiots. The songs chorus is in English while the rest of song is in Hindi (most of the girls native language). As we sang the chorus, many of the girls recognized the song and returned with a verse from the song in Hindi. As we sang, an immediate connection formed between us and the girls. The Kingdom of God was in that home, we were singing with girls who we had never met. We were experiencing joyous singing with girls who not only live 8,000 miles away, but that have a past that makes joy appear to be a long shot.

After we sang, everyone was instructed to pair up for the next activity. In this activity each girl would receive a pad of paper and colored pencils to draw their partners face. I paired up with a girl named Anjani (not her real name). Anjani wore a purple kirta with an orange scarf and had a beautiful, simple smile.

As partners around the room began to settle in, the laughter began. From pair to pair laughs were exchanged. Soon Anjani and I would be joining the laughter. I began to draw her face with as much precision as my ability allowed and I have to admit, it wasnt bad. As she saw my drawing however, she began to laugh at my inability to draw. Soon she began to draw me and she too began hysterically laughing. As the only natural reaction, I also began laughing.

It was at this point that something very interesting happened. I felt connected to Anjani as if she were my family, as if we had known each other for years. If there is evidence for the existence of God here it is: two strangers from totally different backgrounds, after three minutes of drawing each others face, sense a family like connection through laughter, as if they were of the same father.

I began to try speaking with Anjani, but she did not understand me. Soon the girls around me told me that she knew no English. After some urging by her peers however, she practiced what she did know by saying, Hello, my name is Anjani, I am seventeen years old. I responded, Hello, my name is Ben, I am seventeen years old.

There were many giggles exchanged between girls as I announced my age. I laughed as well while internally many emotions rushed through me. Here I was in the presence of a girl my age that had been rescued from the sex trade. She had probably been raped on a daily basis for a period of time and there she was laughing. I,on the other hand, have had a very different past and speak a foreign language, but we shared that moment, connected in what I believe was the Holy Spirit.

So what was India like? My experience was hope in the midst of apparent hopelessness, joy in the midst of brokenness, beauty in the midst of seeming ugliness, and mostly God redeeming his people.
